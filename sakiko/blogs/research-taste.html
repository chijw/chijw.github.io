Notes on Research Taste, After Long Conversations

March 2026

Most research ideas do not fail because they are too small. They fail because they are too vague, too detached from a concrete bottleneck, or too eager to look novel before they become useful.

After enough long conversations around papers, project directions, and half-formed intuitions, one thing becomes clearer to me: research taste is partly the ability to notice where friction actually lives. A good problem is not merely a sentence that sounds new. It is a place where existing methods repeatedly become awkward, inefficient, brittle, or conceptually unsatisfying.

That distinction sounds simple, but it changes how one reads papers. Instead of only asking whether a method is impressive, I think it is often more revealing to ask what pressure forced the method into existence. Was there a real mismatch between the problem and the available tools? Was there a hidden assumption in prior work that quietly limited progress? Did the authors identify a constraint that other people had simply learned to tolerate?

I suspect this is why some ideas keep returning in different forms. They are not fashionable by accident. They persist because they are anchored to a structural need. In contrast, many ideas that appear exciting for a moment dissolve quickly once the surrounding novelty wears off.

This note is only a placeholder for now. Later, I would like to write more concretely about how repeated discussion sharpens the sense for promising directions: which ideas feel deep rather than decorative, which evaluations reveal substance rather than choreography, and how one gradually learns to distinguish clean pressure points from merely crowded topics.
